There are certain experiences during your academic journey that stay with you long after the semester ends. For me, working on my first database project was exactly that kind of experience. It was the moment when everything I had been learning throughout the semester — normalization, SQL, ER diagrams, relational algebra — came together in one real and meaningful task. And the result was something I am genuinely proud of.

## Choosing the Right Dataset

The first step was selecting a dataset to work with. My group mate and I spent time exploring different options on Kaggle, going through various datasets and thinking about which one would best suit our project requirements. We wanted something that was interesting, had enough depth to work with, and could be meaningfully structured into a database.

Once we had made our decision, we presented our selected dataset to Dr. Bilal Ahmad for approval. That step was important to me — not just as a formality, but because getting feedback from someone with his level of expertise meant we were moving in the right direction. Dr. Bilal Ahmad is not only our Database Systems instructor but also an expert in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning. He trains ML models on real world problems and consistently emphasizes the importance of working with high quality, well structured data. His approval gave us the confidence that our dataset was worth building on. You can explore his research and professional work on his [LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/), [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en), and [Facebook page](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm).

## Designing and Normalizing the Database

Once the dataset was approved, we began the actual work. The first major task was converting the raw data into a properly normalized database structure. We applied the normalization techniques we had learned — moving through 1NF, 2NF, and 3NF — to reduce redundancy and organize the data into clean, efficient tables.

This process was more challenging than I had expected. Raw data from real world sources is rarely perfectly organized. There were inconsistencies to resolve, relationships to define, and decisions to make about how to structure the tables. Every decision required us to think carefully about data integrity and long term usability. By the time we finished designing the database, I had a much deeper appreciation for why good database design matters so much.

## Writing SQL Queries

After the database was built, we started writing SQL queries to retrieve and manipulate data according to our project requirements. This was one of the most satisfying parts of the entire project. Seeing a well written query pull exactly the right information from a database — quickly and accurately — made everything we had learned feel real and powerful.

We wrote queries to filter data, join tables, calculate results, and extract meaningful insights. Some queries were straightforward. Others required multiple joins and nested conditions that took time and careful thinking to get right. But every time a complex query worked correctly, it reinforced how much I had grown since the beginning of the semester.

## Training a Machine Learning Model

The project went beyond database design and SQL. We also trained a Machine Learning model using the processed data from our database. This was the part of the project that excited me most — combining what we had learned in Database Systems with the machine learning concepts we had explored earlier.

Seeing how structured, well organized data feeds directly into a machine learning model gave me a completely new perspective on why database design matters. Poorly structured data produces unreliable models. Clean, normalized data produces better results. That connection between databases and machine learning is something Dr. Bilal Ahmad had mentioned throughout the semester, and actually experiencing it firsthand made it unforgettable.

## Building the Frontend and API

To make the project truly complete, we designed a frontend interface using HTML and JavaScript. We then connected this frontend to our trained machine learning model through FastAPI by creating an API. This integration allowed users to interact with the system through a simple interface and receive real results from the trained model.

Building that connection between the frontend, the API, and the machine learning model was one of the most technically challenging things I had done in my first year. There were moments of frustration when things did not connect properly. But working through those problems with my group mate, testing different approaches, and finally seeing the complete system work end to end was one of the most rewarding moments of my entire academic journey so far.

## What This Project Taught Me

Looking back, this project was the most valuable learning experience of my second semester. It gave me hands on experience with databases, SQL, machine learning, APIs, and frontend development — all in one project. More importantly, it showed me how different technologies work together to create a complete solution.

I started the semester learning what a database was. I ended it by building a full system that combined a normalized database, SQL queries, a machine learning model, and a working frontend. That growth, in just one semester, is something I will carry with me throughout the rest of my Computer Engineering degree.
