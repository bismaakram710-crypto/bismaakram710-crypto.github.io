When I first heard about database normalization in my Database Systems course, I was genuinely curious about the concept. The word “normalization” sounded technical, but as soon as I started learning it, I realized that it was actually based on simple logic. Unlike many new topics that seem difficult at first, normalization immediately caught my attention because it focused on organizing data in a better and more efficient way.

At the beginning, I used to think that creating a database was simply about storing information in tables. However, while studying normalization, I learned that a poorly designed database can create duplicate data, inconsistencies, and unnecessary complexity. This was the first time I understood why database design is just as important as the data itself.

As I moved through First Normal Form (1NF), Second Normal Form (2NF), and Third Normal Form (3NF), I started seeing how large and confusing tables could be transformed into smaller, well-structured tables. Initially, identifying partial dependencies and transitive dependencies was a little confusing, but after practicing different examples, the concepts became much clearer. Every normalization step had a purpose, and once I understood the logic behind it, applying the rules became much easier.

One of the biggest lessons I learned from normalization was how to think systematically. Instead of memorizing rules, I learned to analyze data carefully and identify where redundancy existed. I also learned how proper database design improves data integrity, reduces storage waste, and makes future updates easier.

Looking back, normalization was one of the most interesting topics I studied in Database Systems. It not only improved my understanding of database design but also strengthened my logical thinking skills. By the end of the topic, I was able to look at a table and identify how it could be improved, which gave me a sense of confidence and showed me the importance of designing databases correctly from the very beginning.
