There are moments in your academic journey that completely change the way you see a subject. For me, that moment came when we started studying Machine Learning in our course. I had heard the term before — Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence, Deep Learning — these words sounded impressive but also intimidating. I had no idea what they actually meant or how they worked. When I first opened my notes on Machine Learning, I will be honest — it felt dry, technical, and overwhelming. The definitions were heavy, the concepts seemed abstract, and I genuinely wondered if I would ever understand any of it.


When the topic of Machine Learning was first introduced, my initial reaction was confusion. The textbook definitions talked about algorithms, models, training data, and predictions — none of which made immediate sense to me. I tried reading through the material on my own and found myself going over the same paragraphs again and again without truly understanding what was being said. It felt like the kind of subject that was meant for people who were already experts, not for a first year Computer Engineering student who had only recently learned what a variable was.

I remember sitting in class thinking that this was going to be one of those topics I would just memorize for the exam without ever really understanding. That feeling of disconnection from the subject matter is something many students experience, and it is one of the most discouraging things about learning something new and complex. Machine Learning, at first glance, felt like it belonged to a world far beyond where I was.


Then our professor stepped in — and everything changed. Dr. Bilal Ahmad is not just a professor who teaches Machine Learning from a textbook. He is an active researcher and expert in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning. He has spent years training ML models on problems that have real value in the world. One of the things he emphasizes most is using medical datasets for training models, because medical data is collected with high precision and accuracy — which makes it ideal for building reliable and meaningful AI systems.

When someone with that level of real world experience teaches you a subject, the entire energy of the classroom shifts. Dr. Bilal Ahmad did not just explain what Machine Learning is — he showed us why it matters. He connected every concept to actual problems being solved in the real world right now. He explained how ML models learn from data the same way humans learn from experience. He used examples that made sense, asked questions that made us think, and created an environment where curiosity was more important than memorization. You can explore his research and professional background on his [LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/).

The transformation in how I felt about Machine Learning happened gradually but powerfully. What had seemed like a dry and technical subject slowly became one of the most interesting things I had ever studied. I started to see Machine Learning not as a collection of complicated formulas but as a way of teaching computers to recognize patterns, make decisions, and improve over time — just like we do as humans.

I found myself genuinely excited to attend those classes. I looked forward to finding out what new concept we would explore, what real world application Dr. Bilal Ahmad would connect it to, and what questions would come up during the discussion. For the first time in my university life, I was not studying a subject just to pass an exam — I was studying it because I actually wanted to understand it.


Studying Machine Learning also taught me something valuable about the learning process itself. Just like a machine learning model improves with more data and more training, a student improves with more exposure and more practice. The early confusion I felt was not a sign that I was not capable — it was simply the beginning of the training process. Every concept I struggled with, every question I asked, and every example I worked through was adding to my understanding and moving me closer to clarity.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad often reminds us that the best ML models are not built overnight. They require careful preparation of data, thoughtful selection of algorithms, and multiple rounds of training and evaluation. The same is true for students. We need time, the right guidance, and the willingness to keep going even when things feel difficult.


Machine Learning has now become one of the subjects I am most excited to explore further in my Computer Engineering degree. What started as something that felt boring and out of reach has become a field I genuinely want to build skills in. I now understand why Dr. Bilal Ahmad is so passionate about it — because Machine Learning is not just a technical tool. It is a way of solving real problems, improving lives, and pushing the boundaries of what technology can do.

I am grateful that I had a professor who did not just teach the subject but made me fall in love with it. That is the kind of teaching that stays with you long after the semester ends.
