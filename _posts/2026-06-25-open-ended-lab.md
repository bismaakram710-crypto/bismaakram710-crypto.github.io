Not every experience in university is a success story. Some experiences are difficult, embarrassing, and painful — but those are often the ones that teach you the most. My open ended lab in Programming Fundamentals was exactly that kind of experience. It was without a doubt the worst moment of my entire first semester, and looking back, it is also one of the most important lessons I have ever learned.

## What Was the Open Ended Lab?

Towards the end of our first semester, Dr. Bilal Ahmad assigned us an open ended lab. Unlike regular lab sessions where you follow a set of given instructions, an open ended lab requires you to think independently, apply everything you have learned, and solve a problem on your own terms. It is designed to test not just your technical knowledge but your ability to think like an engineer.

For this lab, Dr. Bilal Ahmad assigned each student a dataset to work with. The task was to train a Machine Learning model on that dataset — similar to what we had done for our project, but this time individually and under lab conditions. My assigned dataset was related to kidney disease. It was a medical dataset, which made it particularly significant. Dr. Bilal Ahmad often emphasizes the importance of medical datasets in his own research because they are collected with high precision and accuracy, making them ideal for training reliable ML models.

## The Problem — I Was Not Prepared

Here is the honest truth — I was not prepared for that lab. I had not reviewed the relevant concepts thoroughly enough. I had not practiced working with datasets on my own. I had relied too much on the support of my partner during the project and had not built enough independent confidence in my own abilities. When I sat down in that lab alone, with a kidney disease dataset in front of me and a blank screen waiting for my code, I realized very quickly that I was in trouble.

The things that had seemed straightforward during our group project suddenly felt unfamiliar when I had to do them alone. I struggled to remember the steps, made mistakes in the code, and could not get my model to work properly. Time was passing and I was falling further and further behind. I looked around and saw other students working, and that familiar feeling of being left behind came rushing back — the same feeling I had experienced on my very first day of Programming Fundamentals class.

## The Worst Moment of My First Semester

By the end of the lab, my performance was poor. I had not been able to complete the task the way I should have. Walking out of that lab, I felt a deep sense of disappointment in myself. I had been given a meaningful dataset — kidney disease data that could potentially be used to help identify patients at risk — and I had not done it justice. That feeling stayed with me for a long time.

This was my lowest point in the first semester. Not the missed quiz, not the midterm result — this was the experience that hit me hardest. Because unlike those other moments, this one felt entirely preventable. I had the knowledge somewhere in my mind. I had attended the classes, done the exercises, and worked on the project. But I had not consolidated that knowledge well enough to perform independently under pressure.

## What I Learned From My Worst Experience

As painful as that lab was, it gave me clarity that nothing else could have. It showed me exactly where my weaknesses were. I had been learning alongside my partner without making sure I could do things on my own. I had been understanding concepts in class without practicing them enough independently. And I had underestimated the importance of preparation for individual assessments.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad always says that in the real world of AI and Machine Learning, you cannot always rely on someone else. Whether you are training a model on medical data or solving any other real world problem, there will be moments when you have to stand alone and deliver. That open ended lab was my first taste of that reality — and I was not ready for it.

But I made a promise to myself after that day. I would never walk into an assessment unprepared again. I would practice independently, build my own confidence, and make sure that the next time I faced an open ended challenge, I would be ready for it.

## Moving Forward

That kidney disease dataset deserved better from me. Medical data represents real patients, real conditions, and real lives. Dr. Bilal Ahmad has built his research career around using such data to create models that have genuine value in the world. The least I could do as his student was to take it seriously — and that day, I did not.

But failure, when you learn from it honestly, is one of the most powerful teachers there is. My open ended lab was my worst experience of the first semester. It was also one of the most important.
