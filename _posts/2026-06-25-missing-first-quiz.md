There are some moments in your university life that stay with you not because they were great, but because they taught you something important. For me, one of those moments came very early in my first semester — when I missed the very first quiz of my university life. It was a small event in the larger story of my academic journey, but the feelings it brought up and the lesson it left behind have stayed with me ever since.


It was the first quiz of the semester in Programming Fundamentals — the course taught by Dr. Bilal Ahmad at UET Lahore, Faisalabad Campus. I had been looking forward to attempting it because it was my very first university quiz and I wanted to start strong. But that day, heavy rain made it impossible for me to travel to university. I was stuck at home, watching the clock, knowing that my classmates were sitting in that exam hall while I was helpless.

The feeling was terrible. Missing the very first quiz of my university life felt like starting a race and falling before even reaching the starting line. I kept thinking about how everyone else was there attempting it and I was not. It was frustrating, disappointing, and honestly a little embarrassing — even though it was completely out of my control.


When I came back to university, I found out something surprising. Dr. Bilal Ahmad had decided not to count that quiz. Many students had attempted it, but he chose not to consider it in the final grading and announced that another quiz would be taken later instead. For a moment, I felt a wave of relief wash over me. The thing I had been worried about was no longer going to affect my grade.

But that relief did not last long. Even though the quiz was replaced, something about missing it stayed with me. I realized that the anxiety I felt that day — the helplessness of not being able to show up when it mattered — was a feeling I never wanted to experience agai

That rainy day taught me something that no quiz result ever could. Dr. Bilal Ahmad always emphasizes consistency and discipline in his teaching. As an AI and ML expert who trains models on real world data, he often tells us that consistency in data collection leads to better models — and the same principle applies to students. Consistent effort, consistent attendance, and consistent preparation are what separate good students from great ones.

Missing that first quiz because of rain made me realize that I needed to take my responsibilities more seriously. I could not always predict what would happen, but I could make sure that I was always as prepared as possible. From that day forward, I made a promise to myself — to show up, to prepare in advance, and to never let circumstances catch me off guard if I could help it.
