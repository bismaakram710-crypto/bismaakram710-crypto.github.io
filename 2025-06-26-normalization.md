When I first heard about database normalization in my Database Systems course, I was genuinely curious about the concept. Unlike some topics that initially seem complicated or difficult to relate to, normalization immediately felt logical and practical. The idea of organizing data efficiently and reducing unnecessary repetition made perfect sense to me.

At first, I thought normalization would involve complex rules that would be difficult to remember. However, as I learned more about it, I realized that the concept is actually based on simple logic. The main purpose of normalization is to organize data in a way that reduces redundancy and improves the overall structure of a database. Once I understood this objective, the different normal forms became much easier to follow.

What I found most interesting was how each step of normalization had a clear purpose. Through First Normal Form (1NF), Second Normal Form (2NF), and Third Normal Form (3NF), I learned how large tables can be transformed into smaller and more organized structures. The more examples I practiced, the more comfortable I became with applying these rules.

Another reason I enjoyed learning normalization was its connection to real-world systems. I realized that poorly designed databases can create duplicate data and inconsistencies, while well-structured databases are easier to manage and maintain. Looking back, normalization was one of the database concepts that strengthened my logical thinking and helped me appreciate the importance of good database design.
